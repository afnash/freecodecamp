Nikola Tesla

The man beyond our time

Tesla with his wireless power transmitter (a representation)

  • 1856: Born on July 10 in Smiljan, Austrian Empire (modern-day Croatia), into a Serbian Orthodox family; his father was a priest, and his mother, an inventor of household devices, inspired his interest in innovation.
  • 1870s: Enrolled at the Austrian Polytechnic in Graz to study electrical engineering, where he first encountered the concept of alternating current (AC), but left school without graduating.
  • 1881: Worked as a chief electrician in a telegraph company in Budapest, where he conceptualized the rotating magnetic field, a foundation for AC motors.
  • 1884: Moved to the United States and joined Thomas Edison's company, but differences over DC versus AC power systems led to their parting ways.
  • 1887–1888: Developed and patented the AC induction motor and polyphase AC system, which he licensed to George Westinghouse, sparking the "War of the Currents" against Edison’s DC system.
  • 1891: Became a naturalized U.S. citizen and invented the Tesla coil, which remains a cornerstone in radio technology.
  • 1893: Played a key role in demonstrating the safety and efficiency of AC at the World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago, solidifying its adoption as the standard for electricity distribution.
  • 1898: Pioneered wireless communication by demonstrating a radio-controlled boat, a precursor to modern remote control technology.
  • 1900s: Worked on ambitious projects like Wardenclyffe Tower, aiming to transmit wireless power worldwide, but funding issues led to its abandonment.
  • 1920s–1930s: Focused on visionary but speculative ideas like particle-beam weaponry (dubbed the "death ray") and free energy systems.
  • 1943: Passed away on January 7 in New York City at age 86, impoverished but celebrated for his groundbreaking contributions to electrical engineering, wireless communication, and visionary science.
